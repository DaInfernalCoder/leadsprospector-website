You already know hospital sales are slow. What most medtech founders haven't done is convert that timeline into a dollar figure that shows up on their P&L.

A dedicated hospital sales rep in the US costs $150,000–$200,000 in total compensation. The average medtech rep closes 3–5 health system deals per year — in a good year. That means you're spending $35,000–$65,000 in rep cost alone per closed logo, before you account for travel, solution engineer support, legal review, contracting delays, and executive time.

Multiply that by your active pipeline. If you have 8 deals in motion, each at month 7 of a 14-month cycle, you've already consumed more than half your pipeline investment — with no revenue to show for it.

The problem isn't that hospital sales are hard. It's that most teams haven't measured the true cost of their current motion — so they can't compare it against alternatives, identify where deals are bleeding, or make the case internally for changing how they go to market.

The calculator in the next tab turns your actual numbers into a single figure: what each closed logo costs you in runway. If that number surprises you, it's supposed to.

Benchmark

Why partner-sourced deals outperform

The lowest cost-per-logo in medtech consistently comes from one source.

Partner-sourced deals — where a pre-existing relationship between the introducer and the health system buyer already exists — consistently deliver cost-per-logo 60–75% lower than cold direct sales.

The mechanism is simple: procurement velocity. A buyer who receives a warm introduction from a trusted network contact moves 40–60% faster through the evaluation process. Clinical champions are identified in the first meeting. Legal review gets accelerated. The cycle compresses before it starts.

The target benchmark: cost-per-logo under 25% of ACV. If you're currently running above 50%, your current sales motion has a structural problem — not a headcount problem. Hiring more reps into the same motion scales the cost, not the efficiency.

Most medtech teams at the direct sales stage run 40–80% — meaning the first year of a contract barely covers acquisition cost. The companies that break out of that range do it by changing how they get in the room, not how hard they push once they're there.
